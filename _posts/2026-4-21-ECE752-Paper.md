## Abstract

For decades, the gap between compute capability and memory bandwidth has widened exponentially. Historically, the industry addressed these issues by making advancements in processing to hide the memory latency, to limited success. Recent research shows that leveraging photons in waveguides and memory storage devices versus electrons in traditional technologies has the potential to, at scale, operate at frequencies upward of 10 GHz, outpacing the performance of any single processor to date.

This paper explores the broader potential of optical memories by analyzing the performance of optical caches and optical main memories across various processing architectures. Our findings indicate that while optical main memories exhibit exceptional performance, their large physical size makes them impractical for modern computing systems. Conversely, optical caches strike an optimal balance between size and performance, outperforming existing memory technologies. We further investigate strategic placements for optical caches within the computing stack, highlighting their potential to reshape the memory hierarchy.
